In the opinion of Faegre Drinker Biddle & Reath LLP , Indianapolis, Indiana, Bond Counsel, under existing 
law, interest on the Series 2022D  Bonds is excludable from gross income under Section 103 of the Internal Revenue 
Code of 1986, as amended (the “Code”), for federal income tax purposes .  This opinion relates only to the exclusion 
from gross income of interest on the Series 2022D  Bonds for federal income tax purposes under Section 103 of the 
Code and is conditioned on continuing compliance by the Bond Bank and the Qualified Entity with the Tax Covenants 
(hereinafter defined).  Failure to comply with the Tax Covenants could cause interest on the Series 2022D  Bonds to 
lose the exclusion from gross income for federal income tax purposes retroactive to the date of issue .  In the opinion 
of Faegre Drinker Biddle & Reath LLP, Indianapolis, Indiana, Bond Counsel, under existing law, interest on the Series 
2022D Bonds is exempt from taxation in the State  for all purposes, except for the financial institutions tax .  See the 
form of opinion of Bond Counsel contained in APPENDIX F. 
The Code imposes certain requirements which must be met subsequent to the issuance of the Series 2022D  
Bonds as a condition to the exclusion from gross income of interest on the Series 2022D Bonds for federal income tax 
purposes.  The Bond Bank and the Qualified Entity will covenant not to take any action, within its power and control, 
nor fail to take any action, with respect to the Series 2022D  Bonds that would result in the loss of the exclusion from 
gross income for federal income tax purposes of interest on the Ser ies 2022D Bonds pursuant to Section 103 of the 
Code (collectively, the “Tax Covenants”).  Each of the Indenture and the Qualified Obligations Ordinance and certain 
certificates and agreements to be delivered on the date of delivery of the Series 2022D  Bonds establish procedures 
under which compliance with the requirements of the Code can be met.  It is not an event of default under the Indenture 
if interest on the Series 2022D  Bonds is not excludable from gross income for federal tax purposes or otherwise 
pursuant to any provision of the Code which is not in effect on the issue date of the Series 2022D Bonds. 
The interest on the Series 2022D Bonds is not a specific preference item for purposes of the federal alternative 
minimum tax. 
Indiana Code 6-5.5 imposes a franchise tax on certain taxpayers (as defined in Indiana Code 6-5.5) which, in 
general, include all corporations which are transacting the business of a financial institution in the State.  The franchise 
tax will be measured in part by interest exclud ed from gross income under Section 103 of the Code minus associated 
expenses disallowed under Section 265 of the Code.

General Information of the City of Indianapolis, Indiana 
 
The City of Indianapolis (the “City”) was originally incorporated in 1832. It is the largest city in the State of Indiana 
(the “State”) and the 17th largest city in the nation with an estimated 2020 census population of 887,642 and a 
metropolitan area population of over two million people. The City encompasses a land area of 361 square miles. The 
City, located at the geographic center of the State, is the State capital and also serves as its physical, economic, and 
cultural capital. The City has a stable and diversified economy with employment rates and income levels consistently 
above the national averages. 
 
On January 1, 1970, the governments of the City and that of Marion County (the “County”) were unified and their 
form of service delivery consolidated, thereby extending the City’s boundaries to generally coincide with those of the 
County. Four municipalities (Beech Grove, Lawrence, Speedway, and Southport) located within the County 
boundaries were specifically excluded from most functions of the consolidated City by the consolidating act. The 
consolidated government provides for a Mayor and a 25–member legislative council. The City-County Council of the 
City and County (the “City-County Council”) consists of 25 councilors elected from single-member districts. Because 
the Mayor’s powers extend to the entire County, residents of the Town of Speedway and the Cities of Beech Grove, 
Lawrence, and Southport, the municipalities not affected by the reorganization, vote for the Mayor as Chief County 
Executive.

1965.” In 1973, the Qualified Entity was formed by a city ordinance to acquire, provide and maintain an urban mass

Consolidated City of Indianapolis-Marion County Report Entity. The Qualified Entity is governed by a seven-member 
Board of Directors  (the “Board”), which provides broad policy and financial decisions, setting direction for 
management. To affect these policies, the Board meets monthly in public session. These meetings also include 
executive management presentations regarding the operational and financial status of the Qualified Entity. The Mayor

Local Income Tax (LIT) Revenues 
In 2015, the General Assembly enacted P.L. 243-2015, to consolidate and simplify the various local 
income tax laws into a uniform law and to transition each county from the “former taxes” to a single local 
income tax governed by IC 6-3.6 (the “LIT Statute”).  The LIT Statute, which became effective 
July 1, 2015, provides that the total combined local income tax rate in effect in a county on May 1, 2016, 
under the former statutes continue in effect after that date and will be treated as taxes imposed under 
the LIT Statute.  
In the County, the total income tax rate is 2.020% on the adjusted gross income of local taxpayers.  
Under the LIT Statute, the County’s adopted LIT for 2022 consists of: (i) a property tax relief rate of 
0.039%; (ii) an expenditure rate of 1.731% and; (iii) a special purpose rate at 0.250%.  Per IC 6-3.6-6-2, 
the maximum tax rate that can be imposed under the expenditure rate is 2.75% in Marion County and 
2.50% in all other counties.  Local taxpayers, as it relates to a particular county, are individuals who (i) 
reside in such county on January 1 of the calendar year in which the individual’s taxable year 
commences, or (ii) maintain a principal place of business or employment in such county on January 1 of 
the calendar year in which the individual’s taxable year commences and who does not on that same 
date reside in another county that has a local income tax in effect.  “Adjusted Gross Income” under IC 6-
3-1-3.5(a) is as defined for individuals in Section 62 of the Internal Revenue Code, subject to c ertain 
modifications.   
The LIT Statute allows the County’s adopting body to increase, decrease or rescind a local income tax 
rate.  If there are bonds or leases outstanding that are payable from a tax imposed under the 
expenditure rate or special purpose rate, the adopting body may not reduce the tax rate below a rate 
that would produce one and twenty-five hundredths (1.25) times the total of the highest annual 
outstanding debt service plus the highest annual lease payments plus any amount required under t he 
agreements for the bonds or leases to be deposited in a sinking fund or other reserve, unless: (i) the 
adopting body; or (ii) any city, town, or county pledges all or a part of its share of revenues from the 
expenditure rate or special purpose rate for the life of the bonds or the term of leases  in an amount that 
is sufficient, when combined with the amount pledged by the city, town, or county that issued the bonds 
or entered into the lease, to produce one and twenty-five hundredths (1.25) times the total of the highest 
annual outstanding debt service plus the highest annual lease payments plus the amount required 
under the agreements for the bonds or leases to be deposited in a sinking fund or other reserve.  
The LIT Statute provides that the adopting body must allocate revenues from the expenditure rate to 
either: (i) public safety purposes; (ii) economic development purposes; or (iii) certified shares, and 
provides that the allocation must be described by the use of percentages.  In addition, IC 6-3.6-6-8.5, 
applicable to the County only, provides that the adopting body may allocate revenues to fund the 
operation of a (i) public library, (ii) public transportation corporation, and (iii) public communications 
systems and computer facilities district.  The adopting body may not reduce the proportional allocation 
among these uses if the reduction would allocate less to the payment of bonds or leases for which the 
expenditure rate has been pledged in accordance with law than the amount pledged and payable in that 
year or required under the agreements for the bonds or leases to be deposited in a sinking fund or other 
reserve in that year.  If no portion of the expenditure rate is actually pledged to bonds or leases, the 
adopting body is not restricted in the allocation of additional revenue among the three purposes from 
year to year. 
As it is collected from each county, local income tax revenue is to be deposited in a separate trust 
account for each county within the State General Fund.  Revenue derived from the imposition of the tax 
is to be distributed to the county that imposed it.  The amount that is to be distributed to a county during 
an ensuing calendar year equals the amount of tax revenue that the Indiana State Budget Agency (the 
“Budget Agency”) determines has been: (1) received from that county for a taxable year ending in a 
calendar year preceding the calendar year in which the determination is made; and (2) reported on an 
annual return or amended return processed by the Department of Revenue in the State fiscal year

COVID-19 
Regional, national or global epidemics or pandemics, such as the present outbreak of the novel 
coronavirus (“COVID-19”), could have materially adverse local, regional, national or global economic 
and social impacts. The present outbreak of COVID-19 is adversely impacting local, state, national and 
global economies, as governments, businesses and citizens react to, plan for, and try to prevent or slow 
further transmission of COVID-19. Financial markets in the United States and across the world saw 
substantial declines in March and April 2020. In an effort to lessen the risk of transmission of COVID -19, 
the United States government, state governments, local governments and private industries have taken 
measures to limit social interactions affecting business activities and impacting global, state and local 
commerce and financial markets. The emergence of COVID-19 and the spread thereof is an emerging 
and evolving issue.  
 
On March 6, 2020, Indiana Governor Eric Holcomb issued Executive Order 20- 02 (“EO 20-02”), which 
declared the COVID-19 outbreak in the State to be a public health emergency. On March 11, 2020, the 
World Health Organization proclaimed the COVID-19 outbreak to be a pandemic, and on March 13, 
2020, the President of the United States declared a national emergency in connection with COVID -19. 
The declaration of a public health emergency set forth in EO 20-02 has been renewed multiple times by 
separate Executive Orders, the most recent of which is Executive Order 22-01, which Governor 
Holcomb issued on February 1, 2022. 
 
On June 30, 2021, the Governor issued Executive Order 21-17 (“EO 21-17”), which rescinded all of the 
Executive Orders related to the State’s efforts to contain COVID-19 issued prior to June 30, 2021 and 
thereby eliminated any restrictions or limitations as well as any reporting actions on requirements 
established in any of those Executives Orders. On July 29, 2021, the Governor issued Executive Order 
21-19 (“EO 21-19”), which continues the limited directives set forth in EO 21-17, but provides for a few 
additional requirements, based, in part, on the increase in the seven-day average positivity rate from 
2.1% to 6.3% in July 2021. In particular, EO 21-19 provides that “Hoosiers, whether vaccinated or not, 
should obtain a COVID-19 test and self-quarantine if they or a household member becomes 
symptomatic or has been in close contact with a person who has tested positive for COVID -19, and 
cooperate with contact tracers where warranted.” In addition, it states that the Indiana Department of 
Health has issued measures requiring institutions and congregate care facilities, including K -12 
educational facilities, post-secondary facilities, long-term care facilities, jails, prisons, childcare and 
other congregate settings to report all known COVID-19 cases, to investigate and engage in contact 
tracing and to appropriately isolate those who test positive for COVID-19 and quarantine close contacts.  
EO 21-19 expired on August 31, 2021.  
 
On September 1, 2021, the Governor issued Executive Order 21-24 (“EO 21-24”), which continued key 
directives in EO-17. EO 21-24 also continued the color-coded system to inform individuals about the 
status of COVID-19 in each county and across the State, along with certain restrictions and limitations 
based upon such color-coded system, which had been previously implemented under prior executive 
orders. Under EO 21-24, the Indiana State Department of Health (“ISDH”) will continue to update each 
county’s status under the color-coded system at least weekly. In making the weekly assessments, the 
ISDH will continue to consider the following: (1) the seven-day positivity rate for all tests (percentage of

for features like water conservation, optimized energy performance, use of regional materials, recycling, 
and alternative public transportation access.

The Project 
The Purple Line & 22 Electric Buses 
Approximately half of the bond proceeds will finance the Purple Line and purchase of 22 new electric buses. 
The Purple Line will replace IndyGo’s current Route 39, one of IndyGo’s highest ridership routes. The Purple 
Line will run approximately 15.2 miles and provide a transit connection between Downtown Indianapolis and 
the City of Lawrence. The 22 new electric buses, which cost approximately $29 million, will replace 30 diesel 
buses. The removal of diesel buses from IndyGo’s fleet is a decarbonizing activity and will result in significant 
greenhouse gas emission (“ GHG”) reductions. The Series 2022D Bonds  will also finance infrastructure 
improvements necessary for the Purple Line, including approximately 52,000 feet of storm water sewer 
separation and 12 miles of “Complete Streets” construction. New sidewalks, ADA curb ramps, multi- use 
pathways, pedestrian facilities , and dedicated bus lanes will improve pedestrian access to multi -modal 
transit options. The Series 2022D Bonds finance approximately 25% of the entire $165 million cost of the 
Purple Line project. The Purple Line is expected to be fully operationally in 2024. 
Bus Storage Facility  
Approximately half of bond proceeds will be used to finance construction and renovation of a garage facility, 
located at 2425 West Michigan Street, to store electric buses. The new facility will replace the current garage 
which is 34 years old and in poor condition. The new facility will incorporate rooftop solar panels and wi ll 
include energy efficient features . Proceeds will also fund other aspects of site development at the new 
facility, such as paving, lighting, fencing, heat lamps and drainage improvements.4  
Other Capital Improvements 
Approximately $1.5 million of bond proceeds will finance UV-C disinfectant lighting in IndyGo’s bus fleet and 
facilities. A small portion of proceeds will also finance: the construction, renovation, installation and 
equipping of buildings and a bus facility at the 9503 East  33rd Street campus ; and the installation of a 
computer-aided dispatch system.

Climate Risk and Transition Alignment5  
Mitigation of transition risk requires planning for the necessary structural changes to address climate change 
and the transition to a low carbon economy, with recognition of the risks associated with inaction. IndyGo’s 
bond-financed projects are addressing climate transition risks by financing decarbonizing activities. Projects 
to be financed by the Series 2022D Bonds all directly support the growth and maintenance of a high-quality 
public transportation system with low carbon emissions. Transportation accounts for 29% of GHG emissions 
in the United States and efficient, accessible public transportation systems are critical to transportation 
sector emission reductions.6 As of 2021, IndyGo’s investment in electric buses has reduced GHG emissions 
by 32%. 
The Series 2022D Bonds also finance activities which a lign with the just transition, characterized by the 
equitable inclusion and accommodation of all individuals, with a special focus on disadvantaged groups who 
may be directly or indirectly affected by the structural changes necessary for the transition to a low carbon 
economy. A project that aligns with the just transition works to balance a sustainable society, environment 
and economy and will consider the intersections of racial, socioeconomic, religious, cultural, 
intergenerational or environmental justice. The Series 2022D Bonds support the just transition by financing 
expansion of accessible clean transportation to an economically and racially diverse city in the State of 
Indiana. Those who live at or below the national poverty line  often cannot afford personal transportation, 
such as cars, so public transit serves as an essential mode of transportation for many. Furthermore, IndyGo 
has several programs which offer reduced fares for disadvantaged persons and promote equitable access.   
Process for Project Evaluation and Selection 
IndyGo’s planning documents and mission to provide Indianapolis residents with safe, reliable, and 
accessible mobility guided the process for project evaluation  and established targets for Project success. 
The need for the Bus Rapid Transit project, including the Purple Line, was identified through IndyGo’s capital 
planning process, 2019-2029 Strategic Plan, the Transit Asset Management Plan, the Marion County Transit 
Plan, and the City’s Plan 2020. IndyGo’s participation in the Central Indiana Transit Plan also informed the 
decision to pursue a fully electrified bus fleet. Additionally, the Project advances former Mayor Ballard’s 
long-term vision to reduce the carbon footprint of public transit in Indianapolis.   
The City’s Plan 2020 outlines the implementation of the  Bus Rapid Transit line and details the desired 
measurable outcomes. The Transit Mana gement Plan, which is updated annually,  inventories IndyGo’s 
current operations and communicates recommendations for investments. In the Management Plan, buses 
are regarded as high-priority and the purchase of new buses is based on age, function, and safety of current 
buses. These recommendations are provided to IndyGo’s Planning and Capital Project Committee for further 
prioritization and  then submitted to IndyGo’s seven -member Board of Directors for  final decision.  
Additionally, as a recipient of the Federal Transit Administration funding, IndyGo seeks and includes public 
input regarding capital plans. Public input is required for all fare increases or major operational changes.
