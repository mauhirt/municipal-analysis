UNDS AND ACCOUNTS 
 
Revenue Fund:  All revenues derived from the operation of the Sewage Works and from the collection of sewer rates 
and charges are deposited in the Revenue Fund. This is in accordance with Section 12 of the Bond Ordinance. 
 
Operation and Maintenance Fund:   On the last day of each calendar month, a sufficient amount o f money shall be 
transferred from the Revenue Fund into the Operation and Mainte nance Fund.  The balan ce maintained in the 
Operation and Maintenance Fund shall be sufficient to pay the expenses of operation, repair and maintenance for the

Sinking Fund:  The Sinking Fund consists of the Bond and Interest Account and Reserve Account.  Net Revenues of 
the Sewage Works shall be credited on the last day of each cale ndar month to the Bond and Interest Account in an

equal the interest payable during the next succeeding six (6) c alendar months.  The Reserve Account provides the 
margin for safety against default on the Refunding Bonds.  The balance to be maintained in the Reserve Account shall 
equal but not exceed the least of (i) the maximum annual debt service on the Refunding Bonds, the Outstanding Bonds,

POTENTIAL IMPACTS RESULTING FROM EPIDEMICS OR PANDEMICS,  
SUCH AS THE NOVEL CORONAVIRUS (COVID-19) 
 
General  
 
Regional, national or global epidemics or pandemics, such as the present outbreak of the novel coronavirus (“COVID-
19”), could have materially adverse local, regional, national o r global economic and soci al impacts.  The present 
outbreak of COVID-19 is adversely impacting local, state, national and global economies, as governments, businesses 
and citizens react to, plan for, and try to prevent or slow further transmission of COVID-19.  Financial markets in the 
United States and across the world saw substantial declines in March and April 2020.  On March 6, 2020, Indiana 
Governor Eric Holcomb issued Executive Order 20-02 (“EO 20-02”), which declared the COVID-19 outbreak in the 
State to be a public health emergency.  On March 11, 2020, the World Health Organization proclaimed the COVID-
19 outbreak to be a pandemic, and on March 13, 2020, the President of the United States declared a national emergency 
in connection with COVID-19.  The declaration of a public health emergency set forth in EO 20-02 has been renewed 
16 times by separate Executive Orders, the most recent of which is Executive Order 21-16, which Governor Holcomb 
issued on June 30, 2021.  Such declaration now expires on July 31, 2021. 
  
State-Wide Limitations on Discontinuation of Utility Services 
 
In order to address certain economic impacts of COVID-19, the Governor issued several subsequent executive orders, 
including Executive Order 20-05 on March 19, 2020 (“EO 20-05”).   EO 20-05 provided that providers of gas and 
electric utilities, broadband, telecommunication, water and was tewater services were prohibited from discontinuing 
service to any customer in the State, because these services ar e essential to Hoosiers and Hoosier businesses, 
particularly during this state of public health emergency.  EO 20-05 was extended by other Executive Orders issued 
by the Governor, the most recent of which was Executive Order 20-33 issued on June 30, 2020 (“EO 20-33”), which 
extended this prohibition until August 14, 2020.  The Governor did not extend this prohibition.  Instead, on August 
12, 2020, the Indiana Utility Regulatory Commission (the “IURC”) issued an order, declining to extend beyond August 
14, 2020, the prohibition on utility service disconnections.  This action allows service disconnections to resume after 
August 14, 2020.  The IURC’s August 12, 2020 order does, however, extend by 60 days the requirement in one of its 
prior orders that jurisdictional Indiana utilities not collect late fees, deposits, and disconnection/reconnection fees.  In 
addition, the IURC approved the associated regulatory accounting treatment for COVID-19 costs associated with the 
disconnection moratorium, the waiver of certain fees and the use of expanded payment arrangements to aid customers, 
consistent with the findings from one of its prior orders.  The  IURC’s August 12, 2020 order does, however, allow 
utilities to resume the collection of convenience fees. 
 
State’s Steps to Contain COVID-19 
 
Prior State-Wide Stage Designations.  In addition, in order to take certain steps to increase cont ainment of COVID-
19, the Governor issued various Executive Orders, which include d stay-at-home orders and face covering 
requirements, extended the deadlines for paying certain State and local taxes, directed the closing of State government 
buildings and restricted retail establishments and in-person di ning at restaurants, among other things.  In order to 
continue the mitigation of COVID-19 and to lower the risk of re surgence, the Governor issued several Executive 
Orders, which instituted a measur ed and staggered approach to r eopening businesses and entities.  Five stages were 
outlined, with each ensuing stage being subject to fewer restri ctions and limitations than the previous stage.  Those 
stages were initiated on March 23, 2020 and continued through N ovember 14, 2020, at which time the stage system 
was rescinded by Executive Order 20-48 (“EO 20-48”), issued by the Governor on November 13, 2020.  
 
Current County-Based Measures and Restrictions.  The Governor issued EO 20-48, because, in part, key data points 
or numbers regarding COVID-19, had, as of the date of EO 20-48,  significantly and steadily increased over the past 
several weeks in the State.  In particular, the number of positive cases had doubled every week for the past four weeks, 
hospitalizations were at an all-time high and the seven-day positivity rate went from 3.9% in late September 2020 to 
10.6% as of the date of EO 20-48.  Based on those and other fac tors, EO 20-48 rescinded the stage designations and 
provided that, going forward, the State shall use county-based assessments to determine which measures and 
restrictions are needed to counter the spread or impact of COVID-19 within a particular county.  EO 20-48 indicated 
that the State has implemented a color-coded system to inform Hoosiers about the status of COVID-19 in each county 
and across the State.  EO 20-48 expired on December 13, 2020.

CITY OF ELKHART 
 
SYSTEM OVERVIEW 
 
The City of Elkhart operates a municipal Sewage Works providing  wastewater collection and treatment services to 
approximately 19,500 users.  
 
F
ACILITIES 
 
The City's sewage works facilities were built in 1952. The total length of mains is 366 miles with mains ranging from 
2" to 36". In 1969 (3) primary clarifiers were added, (4) aerat ion tanks were added, (4) final clarifiers were added, a 
chlorine contact tank was constructed and (4) thickeners were added. In 1979, Digester No. 3 was added. In 1982, the 
blower capacity was increased, (2) final clarifiers were added,  Aeration Tank No. 1 was constructed, the dewatering 
building and dewatering process was constructed, and the administration building was added. In 1990, all (5) aeration 
tanks were expanded, (2) final clarifiers were added, de-chlorination facility using sulfur dioxide was added, a cascade 
aerator structure was added, the aerated grit channels were dem olished and a new headworks building with a new 
influent channel was constructed . In 2016, the h eadworks building with mechanical screens and grit removal was 
upgraded to have a 60 MGD capacity and UV disinfection was added to replace chlorine disinfection.

SCHEDULE OF HISTORICAL NET ASSESSED VALUATION
(As Provided by the Elkhart County Auditor's Office)
Real property assessments are annually adjusted to market value based on sales data. The process of adjusting
real property assessments to reflect market values has been termed "trending" by the DLGF.
Real property is valued for assessment purposes at its true tax value as defined in the Real Property
Assessment Rule, 50 IAC 2.4, the 2011 Real Property Assessment Manual ("Manual"), as incorporated into
50 IAC 2.4, and the 2011 Real Property Assessment Guidelines ("Guidelines"), as adopted by the DLGF. In
the case of agricultural land, true tax value is the value determined in accordance with the Guidelines adopted
by the DLGF and IC 6-1.1-4-13. In the case of all other real property, true tax value is defined as "the market
value-in-use of a property for its current use, as reflected by the utility received by the owner or by a similar
user, from the property."
Net assessed valuations represent the assessed value less certain deductions for the blind, as well as tax-
exempt property.
P.L. 180-2016 revises the factors used to calculate the assessed value of agricultural land. This legislation is
retroactive to the January 1, 2016 assessment date and applies to each assessment date thereafter. The revised
factors enacted in the legislation may reduce the total assessed value of agricultural land, which could shift
property tax liability from agricultural property owners to other property owners. In addition, the reduction in
the assessed value of agricultural land may result in a reduction of the total assessed value of a City. Lower
assessed values of a City may result in higher tax rates in order for a City to receive its approved property tax
levy.

expenses are recognized when paid.  Therefore, receivables and p ayables, inventories, 
accrued income and expenses, and utility plant and depreciation , which may be material 
in amount, are not reflected in these statements and these statements are not intended to 
present the overall financial position or results of operation in conformity with generally 
accepted accounting principles.
