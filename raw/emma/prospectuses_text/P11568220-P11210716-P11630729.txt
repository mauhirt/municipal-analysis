Bond Bank (the “Bond Bank” or “Issuer”) on January 24, 2022, and are issued pursuant to the provisions of a Trust Indenture dated 
as of February 1, 2022 (the “Indenture”), between the Bond Bank and The Bank of New York Mellon Trust Company, N.A., as trustee 
(the “Trustee”), and the laws of the State of Indiana, including particularly Indiana Code 5-1.4 (the “Act”). The Trustee will serve as the

Public Improvement Bond Bank Notes, Series 2019 (Stormwater Project), dated March 5, 2019 (the “2019 Notes”) and currently outstanding

improvements to the Qualified Entity’s Stormwater System (as defined herein) (the “2022 Projects”); and (iii) pay all costs incidental to, or 
on account of the issuance of, the 2022 Bonds and the 2022 Qualified Obligations (as defined herein). The 2019 Notes were issued to provide 
funds to (i) purchase the City of Indianapolis, Indiana, Stormwater District Notes of 2019 (the “2019 Note Qualified Obligations”), issued 
by the City of Indianapolis, Indiana (the “City”), for and on behalf of the Marion County Stormwater Management District (the “Qualified 
Entity” or “Stormwater District”), in order to pay for the costs of certain additions and improvements to the Qualified Entity’s Stormwater 
System, and (ii) pay the costs of issuance of the 2019 Notes and the 2019 Note Qualified Obligations. See “FINANCING PLAN.” The Bond 
Bank is a body corporate and politic, separate from the City, and established for the public purposes set forth in the Act. Pursuant to the

(the “Bond Bank”) of its $50,000,000 Bonds, Series 2022C (Stormwater Project) (Green Bonds) (the “2022 Bonds”). 
The 2022 Bonds are authorized by a resolution adopted by the Board of Directors of the Bond Bank on January 24, 
2022, and are issued pursuant to the provisions of a Trust Indenture dated as of February 1, 2022 (the “Indenture”)

each appointed by the Mayor of the City. The Bond Bank has no taxing power.

Obligations”), issued by the City, for and on behalf of the Qualified Entity, in order to pay for the costs of certain

Net Revenues of the Qualified Entity 
The principal of and interest on the 2022 Qualified Obligations is payable from Net Revenues of the

deduction of the Operation and Maintenance Expenses. “Gross Revenues” are defined in the Master Resolution as all 
revenues and income from the Stormwater System, including but not limited to charges, and user charges, but 
excluding revenue from ad valorem taxes, if any. “ Operation and Maintenance Expenses ” are defined in the Master 
Resolution as all reasonable, necessary and current expenses of the Qualified Entity paid or accrued in operating, 
maintaining, and repairing the Stormwater System. The term “Operation and Maintenance Expenses” does not include 
any allowance for depreciation, any debt service, any payments in lieu of taxes, or any operation and maintenance 
expenses paid from property taxes. See APPENDIX B – Summary of Principal Financing Documents, Indenture and 
Definitions.

Collection of Stormwater User Fee 
All properties identified on the property tax records as having an assessable fee are billed semi -annually by 
the Marion County Treasurer’s Office (the “Treasurer’s Office”). The Stormwater User Fee is a separate line item on 
the property tax bills, and bills are due in two payments in May and November. The Treasurer’s Office is provided 
with the amount to be billed by the Qualified Entity’s billing contractor, Wood Environment & Infrastructure Solutions 
Inc. (“Billing Contractor”). The Stormwater District then receives two distributions of the Stormwater User Fee per 
year - in June and December - from the Treasurer’s Office. The amount of the two distributions is roughly equal. See 
“THE STORMWATER SYSTEM - Revenues and Fee Structure.  
 
 Tax-exempt properties having an identified assessable fee are billed on a semi -annual basis directly by the 
Billing Contractor.

Powers and Purposes 
 
The Bond Bank is a body corporate and politic separate from the City. The address of the Bond Bank is Suite 
2342, City- County Building, 200 East Washington Street, Indianapolis, Indiana 46204. Additional information 
regarding the Bond Bank is available on the Bond Bank’s website currently available at 
http://indianapolisbondbank.com/. The Bond Bank was created by the Act for the purpose of buying and selling 
securities of certain qualified entities, including the City, the County, all special taxing districts of the City, all entities 
whose tax levies are subject to review and modification by the City-County Council and certain authorities or entities 
that lease land or facilities to other qualified entities. The Bond Bank was created pursuant to the Act to help the 
qualified entities lower their respective borrowing costs by having the Bond Bank purchase their debt obligations at 
interest rates favorable to the qualified entities. To accomplish its purpose, the Bond Bank may issue bonds or notes. 
The Bond Bank also has general powers which include the power to enter into, make and perform contracts of every 
lawful kind to accomplish its purpose. THE BOND BANK HAS NO TAXING POWER.

THE CITY OF INDIANAPOLIS AND MARION COUNTY 
Governance 
The City is a municipal corporation located in the County. It is the largest city in the State and the fifteenth 
largest city by population in the United States. In 1970, the governments of the City and the County were consolidated 
to form the State’s only consolidated city, which provides services generally throughout the County in which the City 
is located. By the consolidating act, the boundaries of the City were extended to the County line, although the 
municipalities of Beech Grove, Lawrence, Speedway and Southport located within the County were excluded.  
The executive of the City is the Mayor who is elected by all the voters of the County. The Mayor, who may 
serve an unlimited number of four- year terms, has extensive appointive powers and also serves as chief executive 
officer of the County. The executive authority is administered through the following:  Business and Neighborhood 
Services, Indianapolis Metropolitan Police Department, Indianapolis Fire Departmen t, Metropolitan Development, 
Parks and Recreation, and Public Works. 
The Office of Finance and Management operates as the centralized authority in the areas of accounting, 
purchasing, payroll, human resources and budgeting. The Office of Corporation Counse l serves as the professional 
legal staff for the Consolidated City. 
The legislative body of the City and the County is the City- County Council. The City- County Council 
approves the annual budget and any tax levies for the City, the Qualified Entity and other Special Taxing Units of the 
City and the County. The City- County Council is empowered to adopt or to review and modify the budgets and tax 
levies of certain other municipal corporations located within the County. The City- County Council is required to

THE STORMWATER DISTRICT 
General Description 
The Qualified Entity is a special taxing district organized and existing pursuant to the Stormwater Act and 
the Council Ordinance. The Qualified Entity was created by ordinance of the City- County Council in May 2001 for 
the purpose of aiding the City’s effort to remediate stormwater drainage and flooding and to assist in addressing the 
City’s combined sewer overflow problem by improving the stormwater collection and disposal system. Specifically, 
the Qualified Entity’s purpose is to provide for the safe and efficient capture and conveyance of stormwater runoff, 
mitigate the damaging effects of stormwater runoff, correct stormwater collection and conveyance problems and fund 
the activities of stormwater management including, but not limited to, design, planning, regulation, education, 
administration, construction, operation, maintenance, inspection and enforcement activities. The Board of Public 
Works is the governing body of the Qualified Entity. 
The Board of Public Works  
The Board of Public Works consists of seven (7) members. Three (3) members are appointed by the Mayor. 
Three (3) members are appointed by the City-County Council. The Director of the Department of Public Works serves 
as the seventh member and presiding officer. The current director of the Department of Public Works is Daniel Parker, 
who was appointed to such position on June 14, 2017. Members of the Board of Public Works, other than the Director 
of the Department of Public Works, are appointed for one (1) year terms. 
The Board of Public Works has the power to, among other things: sue and be sued in any court having 
competent jurisdiction, contract and be contracted with, and acquire real, personal, and mixed property by deed, 
purchase, gift, grant, devise, lease, condemnation, or otherwise, and dispose of same, and make and adopt appropriate 
regulations, orders, rules, and resolutions and do all things reasonable or necessary to carry out the work and to perform 
the duties under the statute.

THE STORMWATER SYSTEM 
Territory  
 
 Pursuant to the Stormwater Act and the Council Ordinance, the geographic boundaries of the Qualified Entity 
includes all of the County, except for the territory within the City of Beech Grove, the City of Lawrence, the Town of 
Speedway, the City of Southport (as of 2015), and the Town of Cumberland (each of  which are “excluded cities and 
towns” under the Stormwater Act). The territory within the corporate boundaries of the City of Beech Grove, the City 
of Southport, the Town of Speedway, the Town of Cumberland and the City of Lawrence (combined population of  
86,116) are not included within the territory of the Qualified Entity, because each of such entities has either established 
their own stormwater district and/or statutorily withdrawn from the territory of the Qualified Entity pursuant to the 
procedures set forth in the Stormwater Act. See “RISK FACTORS.”  
 24 
 
 
 The Qualified Entity encompasses approximately Three Hundred Seventy- Eight (378) square miles and 
includes approximately Two Hundred Seventy Seven Thousand Seven Hundred Seven (277,707) residential parce ls 
and approximately Twenty-Four Thousand Five Hundred Twenty-Five (24,525) non-residential parcels (these include 
agricultural property, apartment complexes, commercial and industrial properties, schools, churches and municipal 
and federal government faci lities). The system infrastructure consists of drainage outfalls, flood management 
structures (levee and dams), pumping facilities, piped and open channel conveyance systems and water quality 
treatment basins and other facilities. 
 
The Qualified Entity and its predecessors have been maintaining structures and assets since the 1950s. In 
2014, the Qualified Entity completed a separate inventory and valuation of assets using current replacement cost 
methodology. This inventory replacement analysis va lued the Qualified Entity’s assets at approximately $1.1 billion 
(2014 dollars). A new asset inventory is planned for 2024. The Stormwater System includes (a) 1,496 miles of storm 
pipe, valued at over $573 million, (b) 100,000 plus structures including ditches, channels and other structures valued 
at $261 million, (c) 40 miles of levees estimated at $208 million, (d) 7 lift stations estimated at $29 million, and (e) 2 
high hazard dams, worth over $105 million. From 2017-2021 the Stormwater District has completed over $178 million 
worth of capital projects and has approximately $300 million planned from 2022 through 2025. The 2026 capital 
projects are currently under development and will be presented to the City- County Council as part of the budget 
process. 
Management and Operations 
 The Council Ordinance provides that the Qualified Entity’s purpose is to provide for the safe and efficient 
capture and conveyance of stormwater runoff, mitigate the damaging effects of stormwater runoff, correct stormwater 
collection and conveyance problems and fund the activities of stormwater management including, but not limited to, 
design, planning, regulatory compliance, education, administration, construction, operation, maintenance, inspection 
and enforcement activities. 
 
 The Qualified Entity engages an outside consultant to assist with permit compliance monitoring and certain 
administrative functions, including billing and collection. Permit compliance responsibilities include managing the 
City of Indianapolis’ National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (“NPDES”) stormwater permit issued by the 
Indiana Department of Environmental Management (“IDEM”). This NPDES permit requires that the City reduce 
pollutants from the Stormwater System by implementing and complying with a variety of conditions. This is 
accomplished through public education and outreach, standards and specification development and implementation, 
construction permitting, post construction water quality controls, reduction of illicit discharges, proper management 
of City owned facilities and update and implementation of City policies and procedures.  
 
The Qualified Entity also engages an outside consultant to assist in the planning, design and implementation 
of the Stormwater Master Plan. This assistance includes identification of problem drainage areas, project development, 
planning, design and construction assistance. 
 
 Since its inception, the Qualified Entity has retained a third -party contractor to manage the billing and 
collection function s for non- taxable entities. The Treasurer of Marion County bills all taxable properties for the 
Stormwater User Fee once a year with payments being collected in May and November through the property tax 
billing and collection process based on billing data provided by the Qualified Entity.  
 
Revenues and Fee Structure 
Revenues of the Qualified Entity are generated through the assessment and collection of user fees as provided 
for in the Stormwater Act and the Council Ordinance (“Stormwater User Fee”). The Qualified Entity’s current fees 
are assessed on a per parcel basis. The fee for a particular parcel is dependent upon the amount of impervious surface 
area (“ISA”). The ISA is measured by the Qualified Entity utilizing aerial photography. This new rate structure took 
effect on July 1, 2015, and provides for annual $0.05 increases each subsequent January through 2034. Beginning July 
1, 2015, all parcels were subject to a Stormwater User Fee which reflects the measured ISA located on the parcel. 
Each parcel having any ISAs was assessed a fee of One Dollar and Ten Cents ($1.10) per month for each 1,000 square 
feet of ISA on the property. Each 1,000 square feet of ISA on the property is a Base Billing Unit (“BBU”) and BBUs  
 25 
 
are rounded to the next highest whole number for Stormwater User Fee billing purposes. The monthly Stormwater 
User Fee is the product of the rate ($1.45 as of January 1, 2022) and the number of Base Billing Units (BBUs). ISA is 
defined as an area that is either paved or covered with buildings or materials, such that the infiltration of surface water 
into the soil is impeded. As an example, a parcel having 2,700 square feet (3 BBUs) of ISA would pay a Stormwater 
User Fee of $4.35 per month in 2022. Information for 2014 and prior years is available t hrough EMMA.

enforce this by tax sale for taxable and tax-exempt properties by the Treasurer.

Capital Improvement Plan  
 In February 1998, IDEM issued an NPDES Stormwater Discharge Permit to the City of Indianapolis. Under 
the terms of the permit, the City was to develop a stormwater management program designed to improve the overall 
water quality of stormwater runoff. As a result, the Department of Public Works commissioned the development of a 
County-wide stormwater master plan (the “Plan”). 
 
 In 2001, the initial Stormwater Master Plan was issued to address problems and issues related to poor 
drainage, flooding, water quality, stream protection and other environmental impacts. The Plan inventoried and 
prioritized the roughly twelve thousand five hundred (12,500) outstanding stormwater related complaints; ranked the 
severity of problems; developed solution concepts and preliminary cost estimates; and reviewed financing options. 
The report identified approximately Three Hundred Million Dollars ($300,000,000) of projects associated with about 
three hundred fifty (350) projects, ranging from simpler maintenance activities to long- term capital improvement 
projects. The Plan was adopted by the Board of Public Works on July 25, 2001. Subsequent updates to the Stormwater  
 28 
 
Master Plan have continued to identify needs, which have considerably outpaced available funds necessary to address 
such needs. In 2019, the City reported 1,300 problem areas throughout Marion County.  Since 2019 the District, 
through the Department of Public Works, has reduced the earlier identified backlog by the expansion of stormwater 
operations personnel and increased inspection, triage, assignment and execution of stormwater operations 
activities.  During this same period Department personnel enhanced the planning, development of the stormwater 
capital program.  At the end of 2021 the Department reported that approximately 527 identified problems  areas exist 
in Marion County and continue to be processed through both operation and engineering review.   
 
 The Qualified Entity’s 2021-2025 CIP estimates capital improvements of over $300,000,000 will need to be 
made from 2021 through 2025 to comply with current regulatory requirements and to implement the Qualified Entity’s 
improvements to mitigate stormwater runoff management and water quality needs. The Qualified Entity currently 
expects to finance planned capital needs over the next four (4) years from revenues generated from the Stormwater 
System, short term notes, long-term bond proceeds, and funds on hand. The CIP references eight (8) main categories 
of expenditures and includes ‘Over-programming’ in the event projects need to be substituted and/or revenues exceed 
projections.

Withdrawal from Territory of Qualified Entity 
 The territory of the Qualified Entity currently includes all of the County, except for the territory within the 
City of Beech Grove, the Town of Speedway, the City of Southport, the City of Lawrence, and the Town of 
Cumberland (each of which are “excluded cities and towns” under the Stormwater Act). The territory within the 
corporate boundaries of the City of Beech Grove, the Town of Speedway, the City of Southport, the Town of 
Cumberland and the City of Lawrence are not included within the territory of the Qualified Entity, because each of 
such entities had either established their own stormwater district at the time the Council Ordinance was adopted or 
have otherwise statutorily withdrawn from the territory of the Qualified Entity pursuant to the procedures set forth in 
the Stormwater Act. Pursuant to the Stormwater Act, any excluded city or town in the County may, at any point in the 
future, withdraw from the territory of the Qualified Entity pursuant to certain procedures set forth in th e Stormwater 
Act. Most recently, the City of Lawrence elected to withdraw from the jurisdiction of the Qualified Entity, effective 
December 21, 2020, pursuant to the provisions of the Stormwater Act, pursuant to which the City of Lawrence is 
liable for, and required to pay, an allocable portion of the indebtedness of the Qualified Entity that is outstanding as 
of the effective date of its withdrawal. There is no other eligible city or town in the County which may withdraw from 
the territory of the Qualified Entity.

SUMMARY OF BOND RESOLUTION  
OF THE CITY OF INDIANAPOLIS  
STORMWATER DISTRICT 
 
 
The City of Indianapolis, Indiana, Stormwater District (“Qualified Entity”) issued its Stormwater District 
Revenue Bonds of 2022 (Green Bonds) (“2022 Qualified Entity Bonds”) pursuant to the Qualified Entity's Master 
Bond Resolution and Supplemental Bond Resolution (collectively, the “Bond Resolution”). Certai n capitalized terms 
used in this summary are defined at the end of this APPENDIX B. 
 
Source of Payment 
 
The 2022 Qualified Entity Bonds are revenue obligations of the Qualified Entity payable from the Net 
Revenues of the Stormwater System of the Qualified Entity. 
 
Establishment of Funds and Accounts 
 
In the Bond Resolution, the Qualified Entity established and created the following Funds and Accounts and 
the flow of funds is as follows:  
 
(a) Required Flow. All Gross Revenues shall be deposited as received into the Stormwater Maintenance 
and General Expense Fund. Moneys from time to time credited to the Stormwater Maintenance and 
General Expense Fund shall be applied as follows in the following order of priority:  
 
(i) First, semi-annually in June and December, to retain such amounts within the Stormwater 
Maintenance and General Expense Fund to reimburse Operation and Maintenance 
Expenses for the preceding six months of the budget year. 
 
(ii) Second, on the last business day of each June and December, to transfer 6/12 of the 
principal due on the next principal payment date and the full interest due on the next interest 
payment date to the Revenue Bond Interest and Principal Fund to pay the principal of and 
interest on the 2022 Qualified Entity Bonds and any other Revenue Bonds outstanding, all 
fees charged or incurred in connection with the services of a registrar and paying agent and 
fees for credit enforcement. 
 
(iii) Third, on the last business day of each June and December, to transfer all amounts to the 
Revenue Bond Reserve Fund required by any Supplemental Resolution.  
 
(iv) Fourth, on the last business day in June and December, to transfer all amounts to the 
Subordinate Securities Interest and Principal Fund required by any Supplemental 
Resolution authorizing the issuance of Subordinate Securities. 
 
(iv) Fifth, on the last business day of each June and December, to transfer all amounts to the 
Subordinate Securities Reserve Fund required by any Supplemental Resolution authorizing 
the issuance of Subordinate Securities. There are presently none. 
 
(b) Moneys remaining in the Stormwater Maintenance and General Expense Fund, after making the 
deposits provided above, may be used by the Stormwater District for any lawful purposes.  
 
Investment of Funds and Accounts 
 
All Funds and Accounts established under the Resolution shall be held, administered and invested in a manner 
which is permitted by, and consistent with, Indiana law.

The Indianapolis Local Public Improvement Bond Bank (“Bond Bank”) is issuing its Series 2022C 
Bonds (Stormwater Project) (Green Bonds) in the amount of $50,000,000 (the “2022C Bonds”) to

Green Bonds are any type of bond instrument where the proceeds will be exclusively applied to finance or 
refinance, in part or in full, new and/or existing eligible Green Projects and which are aligned with the four 
core components of the Green Bond Principles (International Capital Market Association definition).

The 2022 Bonds will b e used to finance stormw ater and flood mitigation 
projects, to refund outstanding Series 2019 Notes used for similar purposes 
(collectively, the “Projects”), and pay costs of issuance. The 2022 Bonds 
support climate adaptation and resilience planning by addressing increased

are eligible projects as defined by the Green Bond Principles in the project

The City’s Thrive Indianapolis plan, developed in coordination with  the Indianapolis Department of Public

quality. 
To improve Indianapolis’ resilience to climate-related risks, the 2022 Bonds finance projects that incorporate 
innovative approaches to sustainable stormwater management— primarily, nature-based solutions, which

examples. Nature-based solutions financed by the 2022 Bonds include: 
 Hybrid ditches, which use an engineered substrate overlayed with topsoil and vegetation to slow the 
flow of stormwater and naturally improve water quality; 
 City parks that use green space to make room for floodwaters, which not only lessen flood damage 
but also support secondary community benefits; and  
 Daylighting creeks, which is the practice of restoring buried waterways to reestablish more natural 
conditions.    
These best practices, along with the Department’s work to update design standards to accommodate 
anticipated effects of climate change, increase resilience of the stormwater and flood mitigation systems.

Climate Risk and Transition Alignment 
Climate change poses significant systemic risks to US financial systems and municipal issuers. These risks 
may broadly be divided into physical risk and transition risk. Physical risks include effects of climate change 
on physical assets, such as extreme weather events and sea level rise. Transition risk includes market and 
technology risks, reputational risks, policy risks and legal risks. Transition and physical risk factors may 
interact in complex ways. 
Mitigation of transition risk requires planning for the necessary structural changes to address climate change 
and the transition to a low carbon economy, with recognition of the risks associated with inaction. The 
stormwater projects financed by the 2022 Bonds are addressing climate transition risks by maintaining a 
state of good repair in a system that is critical to flood mitigation and water quality improvement.  
The 2022 Bonds also finance activities which align with a just transition , characterized by the equitable 
inclusion and accommodation of all individuals, with a special focus on disadvantaged groups who may be 
directly or indirectly affected by the structural changes necessary for the transition to a low carbon economy. 
A project that aligns with the just  transition works to balance a sustainable society, environment and 
economy and will consider the intersections of racial, socioeconomic, religious, cultural, intergenerational 
or environmental justice. The 2022 Bonds support a just transition by addressing flood mitigation and water 
quality with a focus on disadvantaged groups, including low- moderate- income neighborhoods that may be 
disproportionately affected by the impacts of climate change.

Process for Project Evaluation and Selection 
The Department’s mission to manage a safe and efficient stormwater system and achieve the sustainability 
goals established in the Thrive Indianapolis sustainability plan guide the process for project evaluation and 
selection, as does the Department’s long-term capital improvement planning process , design standards, 
permitting requirements. 
The Department compiles stormwater system needs in a 20- year Storm Water Master Plan  and uses an 
established selection process to score and prioritize projects  for funding. Residential input and complaints 
are important considerations in development of the Plan. The Department and Citizens Energy Group 
collaborate to prioritize projects necessary to address drainage complaints. Additionally, new stakeholders, 
such as forestry and land stewardship groups, have been incorporated into the decision -making process. 
Projects are scored using a weighted point system which considers property  usage, the type of streets 
involved (high use or low use corridors), number of affected proper ties, extent of erosion, water quality 
impacts, cost sharing,  and presence of any ongoing transportation projec t (in order to combine paving 
projects with drainage projects, when possible). Effects on water quality are weighted most heavily in the 
scoring system, and problems located in a combined sewer overflow area or an area without sanitary sewer 
connections are higher priority.  Projects that score high in the evaluation process are added to the 
Department’s Needs Inventory in the Storm Water Master Pla n, and subsequently the four-year Capital 
Improvement Plan. 
The Department uses design standards with best management practices, including green infrastructure and 
low impact development, to ensure effective and sustainable stormwater management and incorporate 
nature-based solutions. Indianapolis’ Stormwater Design and Construction Specifications Manual, and the 
Green Infrastructure supplement, set requirements and guidelines for facilities design. The Department also 
uses selection criteria that prioritize best practices for green infrastructure.  
The Department’s bond-financed activities are also governed by its National Pollution Discharge Elimination 
System permit, which requires the City to implement a stormwater capital program and regulate stormwater 
discharge into surface waters. For certain projects, monitoring and educational activities are required under 
this permit, which provide additional assurance and transparency in the Department’s operations and capital 
project implementation.
